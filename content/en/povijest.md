The Parish of Grohote is the oldest parish on the island of Šolta. The area was inhabited by the ancient Illyrians, and most archaeological remains date from the period of Roman rule. Christianity also spread to this area early, as evidenced by an early Christian basilica from the 5th or 6th century whose floor was decorated with a mosaic similar to those found in the basilicas of Salona. In front of the presbytery an inscription with the name Honorius was found on the mosaic — in all likelihood the benefactor of the church. Early Christian graves were also discovered in the basilica. This basilica was most probably burned in the 13th century, as confirmed by stones in certain places beginning to turn to lime. According to Thomas the Archdeacon, the basilica was burned by pirates from Omiš in 1241. In the 14th century construction of a new Church of Saint Stephen began; it was demolished in 1913 to make way for the present, larger church.

From the very beginning until the end of the 14th century there was probably only one parish on Šolta, with one parish priest who had several assistant priests serving in other villages on the island. The Parish of Grohote is first mentioned in 1483, and shortly afterwards — in 1495 — the Parish of Saint Stephen in Grohote is recorded. At that time the surrounding settlements of Donje Selo and Gornje Selo were part of it; they separated from the Parish of Grohote between 1579 and 1598. Maslinica was not yet inhabited at that time. Around the same period the Parish of Saint Mary in Bori or in Stomorija is also mentioned, preserving the revered memory of a Benedictine monastery and the Church of Our Lady.

The parish house in Grohote was built in 1861 during the pastorate of Fr. Mijo Vučković. It has been repaired on several occasions.

Parish registers of births from 1842 to 1864 and marriages from 1842 to 1873 are held in the State Archives in Split (DAS). Registers of births, marriages and deaths from 1658 to 1793; births from 1793 to 1814 and 1825 to 1842; marriages from 1825 to 1857 (two volumes); and deaths from 1793 to 1842 (two volumes) are held in the State Archives in Zagreb (DAZ).

In 1938 the parish had 1,400 souls; in 2001 there were 639.
